International Women’s Day has become a ritual of corporate statements and pink products. But the original socialist feminists understood that women’s oppression is not an isolated evil – it is integral to capitalism.

In Nigeria, women perform the majority of unpaid domestic work, are overrepresented in the informal economy (often without any protection), and face violence that the state routinely fails to address. These are not cultural problems; they are structural features of a system that devalues all labour that does not produce profit.

A Marxist analysis shows that the first oppressed class was women, and the family form that emerged with private property remains the site of much of that oppression. Abolishing private property is therefore a prerequisite for women’s full liberation.

This does not mean postponing demands for reproductive rights, equal pay, or an end to gender‑based violence. We fight for those demands now. But we understand that they will never be fully secured under capitalism. Only a socialist transformation – where production is organised for human need, not profit – can finally uproot patriarchy.

The Naija Marxists organise women’s caucuses within our study groups and support all struggles against gender oppression. We call on feminist activists to join us in building a revolutionary movement that unites class and gender struggle.
